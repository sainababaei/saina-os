Teams often ask for prioritization when they are missing strategy.

A scoring model can rank items, but it cannot decide what kind of company the product is trying to become. When strategic choices remain unresolved, every feature can be made to look important.

## Prioritization is downstream

Methods such as RICE, MoSCoW or weighted scoring are useful within a chosen direction. They compare options against criteria. They do not create the criteria.

Before scoring features, the organization needs answers to three questions:

- Where will we play?
- How will we win?
- What will we deliberately not do?

Without these choices, prioritization becomes arithmetic over political inputs.

## Features are commitments

Every feature commits more than engineering time. It creates maintenance, support, documentation, user expectation and future compatibility costs. The relevant question is therefore not only whether a feature is valuable, but whether it strengthens the chosen system.

## Use strategic filters

A strong filter might ask:

- Does this deepen our advantage in the chosen segment?
- Does it improve the critical behavior in our model?
- Does it create reusable capability?
- What opportunity does it displace?

These questions make the cost of saying yes visible.

## The discipline of exclusion

Strategy is credible only when it excludes plausible options. “Serve everyone better” is an aspiration, not a strategy. A real choice disappoints someone.

## A better sequence

Start with strategic boundaries. Translate them into product principles. Then use prioritization methods to allocate capacity within those boundaries.

The result is not perfect certainty. It is coherent uncertainty.

That is enough to make better feature decisions.
