Best practice sounds reassuring because it promises a shortcut: someone else has already discovered the right answer. In reality, most best practices are compressed stories with the context removed.

A practice worked somewhere, for a particular team, at a particular stage, under a particular set of constraints. When copied elsewhere, the visible procedure travels. The invisible conditions usually do not.

## Practices are outputs of systems

A weekly planning ritual may look like the reason a company executes well. The deeper cause might be unusually clear ownership, high-quality data and a leadership team willing to stop work. Copying the meeting without those conditions produces ceremony rather than capability.

The same problem appears in product discovery, hiring, growth and strategy. Teams reproduce templates while leaving the decision system unchanged.

## Replace imitation with diagnosis

Before adopting a practice, ask four questions:

- What problem was this practice solving?
- Under what assumptions did it work?
- Which capabilities made it effective?
- What would make it fail here?

These questions turn a borrowed answer back into a design problem.

## From best practice to bounded practice

A more honest phrase is **bounded practice**: a method that is useful within declared conditions.

For example, “two-week sprints” are not inherently good. They can be useful when work can be sliced meaningfully, dependencies are manageable and review cycles benefit from that rhythm. Outside those conditions, the same cadence may create fragmentation and artificial deadlines.

## Principles travel better than procedures

Procedures are often local. Principles are more portable.

Instead of copying a competitor’s prioritization meeting, preserve the principle: scarce capacity should be allocated through explicit trade-offs. Your implementation may be a weekly meeting, a written review or a continuous decision queue.

The point is not to reject all proven methods. It is to stop treating evidence from one environment as a command for another.

## A better operating question

Do not ask, “What do the best companies do?”

Ask:

> What must be true for this practice to work here?

That question preserves curiosity without outsourcing judgment.
